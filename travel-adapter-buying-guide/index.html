How to choose the right travel adapter

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A travel adapter simply changes the shape of your plug to fit a foreign outlet; it does not convert voltage. For 2026, the best choice is a universal model with multiple USB ports and fast-charging support that covers at least three common plug types, and you should check whether your devices are dual voltage before you pack.

What types of travel adapters are there?

Most travel adapters fall into two broad categories: single-country (or single-region) adapters and universal adapters. Single-country adapters are small and inexpensive, designed to work in just one outlet standard, such as the British Type G or the European Type C. Universal adapters include several plug prongs that slide or snap into place, covering dozens of countries with one device. Within universal adapters you will also find grounded (three-prong) and ungrounded (two-prong) versions. Grounded adapters are safer for laptops and other electronics that require a ground connection, but they are slightly bulkier. Some modern universal adapters also add built-in USB-A and USB-C ports so you can charge multiple devices without carrying extra wall chargers.

What features matter most in a travel adapter?

The most important feature is the set of plug types it supports. A good universal adapter should cover at least the four most common standards: Type A (North America/Japan), Type C (Europe), Type G (UK/Ireland), and Type I (Australia/New Zealand). Many globetrotters appreciate adapters that also include Type F (Schuko) and Type J (Switzerland). Beyond plug compatibility, look for the number and speed of USB ports. A high-quality adapter will include at least two USB-A ports and one USB-C port with Power Delivery (PD) for fast laptop charging. Surge protection is a nice-to-have, especially if you plug in expensive electronics. Finally, consider the overall size and shape: a compact, low-profile design fits more easily in a carry-on and is less likely to block adjacent outlets.

Which travel adapter brands are most reliable?

Several brands consistently earn praise from frequent travelers and published reviews. Zendure makes a well-regarded series of compact universal adapters with high-wattage USB-C PD output. Skross (from Switzerland) is known for rugged, well-built adapters that cover many plug types, including grounded versions. Ceptics and Epicka offer budget-friendly universal adapters that still include multiple USB ports and decent build quality. Tessan sells a popular very affordable universal model that is widely available online. When choosing a brand, pay attention to safety certifications. Look for adapters that meet international standards like CE, FCC, or RoHS. Avoid extremely cheap unbranded adapters that may not have proper internal fusing or surge protection.

Do travel adapters convert voltage?

No, a standard travel adapter does nothing to change the voltage. It simply lets your plug fit into a foreign outlet. If your device is not dual-voltage (look for an input rating like ‘100–240V’ on its label), you will need a separate voltage converter or transformer, which is heavy and rarely worth traveling with. Most modern phone chargers, laptops, tablets, and camera battery chargers are dual-voltage and work happily with any outlet from 100 V to 240 V. Small appliances like hair dryers, curling irons, and electric shavers are often single-voltage and will burn out if used through a simple adapter in a 220 V country. For those items, a dedicated voltage converter is required, or you should simply buy a cheap local appliance when you arrive.

How do you choose an adapter for multiple countries?

If you visit several countries on one trip, a universal adapter is the obvious answer because you can switch the plug prongs as needed. Some universal adapters are ‘all-in-one’ with retractable prongs, while others use interchangeable snap-on heads. The retractable style is more compact and harder to lose parts. For very frequent travelers, a premium universal adapter with at least four plug types and high-speed charging is worth the investment. Occasional travelers who only visit one or two countries may prefer a simple single-region adapter because it is smaller and cheaper. Just be sure to check the outlet standards of every destination, some countries use unusual plugs (like Type M in South Africa) that not all universal adapters cover.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use my hair dryer with a travel adapter?

Only if your hair dryer is dual-voltage (rated 100–240 V). Most hair dryers are single-voltage and will be damaged or pose a fire risk when used through a simple adapter in a 220 V outlet. You would need a bulky voltage converter, or it is better to buy a local hair dryer at your destination.

Do travel adapters work with USB-C laptops?

Yes, but only if the adapter has a USB-C port that supports Power Delivery (PD) at a wattage sufficient for your laptop. Many inexpensive adapters only output 15 W or 18 W, which is too slow for most laptops. Look for an adapter that advertises at least 45 W or 65 W USB-C PD for reliable laptop charging.

What is the difference between a travel adapter and a voltage converter?

A travel adapter only changes the shape of the plug. A voltage converter (or transformer) changes the electrical current from 220 V to 110 V or vice versa. Adapters are small, light, and cheap; converters are heavy, expensive, and rarely needed for modern electronics that already accept both voltages.

Are there travel adapters with multiple USB ports?

Yes, many universal travel adapters now include two or more USB-A ports and one or two USB-C ports. Some high-end models even have a total of four to six USB ports, allowing you to charge phones, tablets, earbuds, and a power bank all from one adapter. Just be aware that total power is shared, so charging many devices at once may slow each down.

Should I buy a universal adapter or separate country-specific adapters?

If you travel to multiple countries on one trip, a universal adapter is more convenient and saves space. If you only ever visit one or two countries, a dedicated single-region adapter is smaller and less likely to break. Many travelers keep a universal adapter as their main travel tool and buy a cheap single-region backup for emergencies.

Will a universal travel adapter work in every country?

No universal adapter covers every plug type in the world. Most cover at least six to eight common standards (Types A, C, G, I, and sometimes F, J, L, N). Less common plugs, such as Type M (South Africa), Type D (India), and Type O (Thailand), are often missing. Always check the specific countries on your itinerary against the adapter’s supported types before you buy.

In shortThe best travel adapter for 2026 is a universal model that supports at least the plug types you will encounter, includes fast USB-C PD for laptop charging, and has built-in safety features. Confirm that your devices are dual-voltage so you can skip the heavy converter. A well-chosen adapter costs a few dozen dollars and makes your trip far simpler, just remember to unplug it when you leave the hotel room to save energy and avoid any risk.
